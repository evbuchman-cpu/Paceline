8 Weeks Out
Download the official race guideAid stations, cutoffs, crew-access points. Print it.
Map every crew-accessible aid stationDrive times between them. Most crews lose 20–30 min getting lost.
Confirm crew members + assign rolesChief, Logistics, Nutrition. More on page 5.

4 Weeks Out
Complete your longest training runLast long run ≥ 3 weeks pre-race for adequate recovery.
Finalize and test all gearShoes broken in. Vest dialed. Poles — practice with them.
Build your pacing tableProjected times at each aid station. Share with crew. (See page 3.)
Practice your nutrition planSame calories/hr, same products as training. Zero experiments.
2 Weeks Out
Deliver crew briefing documentEvery crew member gets their own copy: arrival times, roles, needs.
Pack drop bagsLabel clearly. Organize by station. Double-check required gear.
Identify your 3 tightest cutoffsKnow exactly how much buffer you have at each one. Calculate now.
Prep your contingency kitBlister kit, anti-nausea, salt tabs. Crew knows where it lives.
Race Eve + Morning
Nothing new. Zero exceptions.No new shoes, food, gear, or supplements. Zero.
Sleep by 9pm (even if you can't sleep)Horizontal rest is still rest. Two nights before matters most.
Eat your proven pre-race mealHigh carb, low fiber, familiar. 3 hours before start.
Text crew exact meeting spotNot "at the aid station" — the specific location.
Paceline Pro Tip
The two nights before race day matter more than race eve. Most runners sleep poorly the night before — that's normal. Prioritize sleep starting 3 nights out.

Race Day — Miles 1 Through Finish
Pacing Rules That Protect Your Finish
The goal is not to run fast — it's to run slow enough that you can still run at mile 80. These rules separate finishers from DNFs.
Rule 1: Start 20–30 sec/mile slower than feels rightEarly miles feel easy. That's a trap. Your first 20 miles should feel almost embarrassingly slow. Bank nothing. Save everything.
Rule 2: Power hike every significant climb from mile 1Running uphills burns ~3× more energy than hiking at equivalent pace. Walk the ups. Run the downs. Protect your quads for the back half.
Rule 3: Use perceived effort, not paceElevation, heat, altitude, and fatigue make pace meaningless after mile 40. Target effort 5–6/10 for the first half. Never above 7 before mile 60.
Rule 4: Calculate your elevation-adjusted paceAdd 1 min/mile per 100 ft/mile of gain. On steep terrain (>8% grade), add 2 min/mile. Flat pace is irrelevant in mountain ultras.
Rule 5: Never make up time in the first 50 milesAhead of pace at mile 40? Slow down. You haven't earned those minutes — you've borrowed them at 30% interest, due after mile 70.

Night Running (Miles 50–80, typical)
Add 15–20% to pace targets after dark. Depth perception drops, reaction time slows. Plan for this in your aid station arrival projections — most runners underestimate how much night slows them down.
The Mile 62 Trap
Most mid-race DNFs occur between miles 55–70. You're past the halfway euphoria, glycogen is depleted, and you still have a marathon to go. A solid first half buys you the ability to push through this window.

Hours 1–6 (Early Race)
Eat within first 30 minutesYour gut is happy now. Train it to accept fuel while moving well.
Stick to familiar foods onlyGels, chews, or real food from training. Zero experiments.
Alternate solid and liquid caloriesPrevents early GI fatigue from sweet gel monotony.
Stay ahead of hydrationUrine should be pale yellow. Dark = you're already behind.
Hours 6–12 (Mid Race)
Shift toward real foodBroth, potatoes, quesadillas, PB&J. Gut prefers savory over sweet by hour 8.
Increase sodium intakeEspecially in heat. Broth at every crew station from here forward.
Don't skip aid stations2–3 minutes eating while walking beats pushing through hungry.
Hours 12+ (Late Race)
Expect appetite suppression — eat anywayMost runners stop wanting food after hour 10–12. Force 150–200 cal/hr minimum.
Caffeine after midnight (or mile 60+)Coffee, cola, caffeinated gels. Don't start too early.
Warm calories = comfort + functionHot broth, ramen, mashed potatoes. Aids morale and digestion in cold.
Small portions, more frequentlyA nauseated stomach can handle small continuous inputs better.
GI Shutdown Protocol
Stop solid food. Switch to broth + electrolytes only for 30–60 min. Walk. Let stomach settle. Reintroduce with plain crackers. Crew should carry Tums and ginger chews.

Crew Coordination Checklist
Your crew can save your race or cost you it. The difference is almost always preparation. Brief them like they've never crewed before — even if they have.
👑 Chief (Lead Crew)
  • Owns the master pacing sheet
  • Makes go/no-go decisions
  • Manages crew energy and morale
  • Final call on pacer deployment
  • Never lets runner sit >10 min
🗺 Logistics (Navigator)
  • Pre-drives all crew routes
  • Arrives 45 min before runner
  • Owns drop bags, staging area
  • Tracks runner via GPS/app
  • Knows parking, access rules
🍲 Nutrition (Fueler)
  • Knows runner's food preferences
  • Has warm food ready: broth, potato
  • Manages all medical supplies
  • Tracks intake across the race
  • Pushes food even when refused
What Crew Needs to Know
Every crew-access aid stationName, mile marker, your projected arrival time (early/on-time/late).
What you need at each stationDifferent bags, different needs. Create a per-station list, not a generic list.
Your pacer planWho joins when, which miles, and their role (push / support / navigate).
Your drop criteriaWhat conditions justify DNF. Crew must know — so they don't accidentally talk you out when you're just low.
Emergency contacts + medical historyOn paper, in their pocket — not just in a phone.
Aid Station Protocol
Arrive 45 min before projected runner timeNot 15 min. 45. Parking, staging, and orientation all take time.
Stage everything before runner arrivesWarm food ready, shoes accessible, chair out, headlamp charged.
The 10-minute ruleNo sitting for more than 10 minutes unless medical. Runners who sit get stiff; stiff runners quit.
Positive framing only"You're looking strong" even when they're not. Save honest assessments for genuine safety concerns.
Never ask "how are you feeling?"Ask: "What do you need?" Feelings are irrelevant. Execution isn't.
The #1 Crew Mistake
Being at the wrong aid station. Crew-access points differ from pacer-access points. Know the difference. Drive the route in advance if possible.

Know Your Numbers Before Race Day
Cutoff Buffer Strategy
Buffer = Cutoff Time − Your Projected Arrival. Calculate this for every timed cutoff before you toe the line.
Safe: >3 hours buffer
Watch: 1–3 hours buffer
Critical: <1 hour buffer
List every cutoff. Calculate your buffer at each one.Do this before race day. If any cutoff shows <1 hour of buffer, rebuild your pacing strategy.
Identify your 3 tightest cutoffs. Know them by name."I have 90 minutes at Foresthill" is protection. "I'll figure it out" is a DNF waiting to happen.
Fall behind by 20%+ at any cutoff: switch to cutoff mode immediately.Cut aid station time to 3 minutes max. Drop non-essentials. Stop running for enjoyment — run for survival.
Give your crew your cutoff sheet.If you're close to a cutoff and don't know it, your crew should be sending you out the door.
06
Miles 60–80 — The Dark Place
Mental Strategy
Most ultras aren't won or lost on fitness. They're won or lost on the internal conversation between miles 60 and 80.
When: "I want to quit"
Sit for 5 minutes. Eat something. Drink something. Then decide. 90% of quit urges pass with calories and rest. Never decide at a low.
When: "I can't keep going"
"I just need to reach the next aid station." Shrink the goal to 20 minutes. The race doesn't exist — only the next aid station does.
When: Pace has dropped badly
"Forward is forward." Walk it in if you have to. A finish line at 40 hours matters more than a PR you didn't get. Keep moving.
When: Everything hurts
"Is this suffering or danger?" Suffering is expected. Danger means stop. Know the difference before you start.
Pre-Race: Write Your Why
Before race day, write down — on paper — your three reasons for being there. Put it in your vest. At mile 70 when your brain is irrational, reading your own handwriting is more powerful than any mantra.
